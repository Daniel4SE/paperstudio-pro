# Humanities Journal Formatting & Writing Conventions

## Overview

Humanities papers differ fundamentally from STEM papers. They are **argument-driven**, not data-driven. The structure follows a thesis-based narrative rather than IMRAD (Introduction/Methods/Results/Discussion). Evidence comes from close reading, archival sources, and theoretical frameworks rather than experiments.

---

## Citation Styles

### MLA 9th Edition (Literature, Cultural Studies, Film Studies)

Used by: PMLA, Modern Fiction Studies, New Literary History, Cinema Journal, Signs (sometimes)

#### In-Text Citations

```
According to Said, "Orientalism is a style of thought based upon an ontological
and epistemological distinction made between 'the Orient' and 'the Occident'"
(2).

The narrator's unreliability has been widely noted (Brooks 45; Miller 112-14).

In Beloved, Morrison employs a fragmented narrative structure that mirrors the
psychological effects of trauma (42-43).
```

- Page numbers with no "p." prefix
- Author last name only (if not named in sentence)
- Multiple sources separated by semicolons
- No comma between author and page number

#### Works Cited Format

```
Book:
Said, Edward W. Orientalism. Vintage Books, 1978.

Chapter in Edited Volume:
Spivak, Gayatri Chakravorty. "Can the Subaltern Speak?" Marxism and the
    Interpretation of Culture, edited by Cary Nelson and Lawrence Grossberg,
    U of Illinois P, 1988, pp. 271-313.

Journal Article:
Felski, Rita. "Context Stinks!" New Literary History, vol. 42, no. 4, 2011,
    pp. 573-91.

Film:
Parasite. Directed by Bong Joon-ho, CJ Entertainment, 2019.

Archival Source:
Morrison, Toni. Letter to Robert Gottlieb. 3 Mar. 1986. Toni Morrison Papers,
    box 12, folder 3. Princeton University Library.
```

- Hanging indent (0.5 in) for entries
- Italicize titles of long works; use quotation marks for short works
- Publisher names abbreviated (U of Illinois P, not University of Illinois Press)
- Accessed date for online sources: "Accessed 15 Jan. 2026."

### Chicago 17th Edition Notes-Bibliography (History, Philosophy, Art History)

Used by: American Historical Review, Journal of American History, Past & Present, Journal of the History of Ideas, Art Bulletin

#### Footnotes

```
First citation (full):
1. Michel Foucault, Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison, trans.
Alan Sheridan (New York: Vintage Books, 1977), 135.

Subsequent citations (short):
2. Foucault, Discipline and Punish, 200.

Ibid (immediately following same source):
3. Ibid., 201.

Archival source:
4. Thomas Jefferson to James Madison, 6 September 1789, Papers of James
Madison, ser. 1, box 5, folder 12, Library of Congress, Washington, DC.

Journal article:
5. Natalie Zemon Davis, "Women on Top," Society and Culture in Early Modern
France (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 1975), 124-51.
```

#### Bibliography

```
Foucault, Michel. Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison. Translated
    by Alan Sheridan. New York: Vintage Books, 1977.

Davis, Natalie Zemon. Society and Culture in Early Modern France. Stanford:
    Stanford University Press, 1975.
```

- Footnotes use first name then last name; bibliography inverts
- Include full publication details in first footnote citation
- Use "Ibid." only for immediately preceding citation

## Paper Structure (Argument-Driven)

Humanities papers do **NOT** follow IMRAD. The structure is:

```
Title
Abstract (150-200 words, single paragraph, no headings)
[no section numbers typically]

Opening / Exordium
  - Hook: arresting quotation, anecdote, or problem statement
  - Stakes: why this matters now
  - Thesis statement: clear, arguable claim (NOT a summary of findings)

Body Sections (titled thematically, NOT numbered in many journals)
  Section I: Historical/Theoretical Context
    - Situate argument in scholarly conversation
    - Identify gap or problem in existing scholarship
  Section II: Close Reading / Primary Analysis
    - Present evidence through close engagement with texts/archives
    - Build interpretive argument step by step
  Section III: Theoretical Elaboration
    - Extend analysis through theoretical framework
    - Address counterarguments or alternative readings
  Section IV: Broader Implications
    - Connect specific argument to larger questions
    - Gesture toward further research directions

Conclusion
  - Do NOT merely summarize; restate thesis in light of evidence presented
  - Open outward: implications for the field, contemporary relevance
  - End with a resonant final sentence

Notes (endnotes or footnotes depending on journal)
Works Cited / Bibliography
```

## Abstract

- **150-200 words**, single paragraph
- No structured headings (unlike STEM)
- State the argument (thesis), the primary sources/archive, and the contribution
- Do NOT list methods or results — state what you **argue**

```
Example:
This essay argues that Toni Morrison's Beloved (1987) deploys a recursive
narrative structure that formally enacts the psychic processes of traumatic
memory. Through close readings of the novel's three-part architecture, I
demonstrate how Morrison's prose style — its circling repetitions, shifting
focalization, and strategic withholding of information — mirrors the
phenomena that contemporary trauma theory describes as intrusion and
constriction. In conversation with Cathy Caruth's work on traumatic
temporality and Hortense Spillers's theorization of flesh and body, I
propose that Morrison's formal innovations constitute a distinctly Black
feminist poetics of witness that resists both the erasure and the
sensationalization of enslaved experience.
```

## Close Reading Methodology

Close reading is the foundational method of literary humanities. Structure each close reading passage as:

1. **Quotation**: Present the textual evidence
2. **Observation**: Identify the formal/linguistic feature (diction, syntax, imagery, rhythm)
3. **Interpretation**: Explain what this feature does — its effect, implication, or meaning
4. **Connection**: Link to your larger argument or theoretical framework

```
Example:
Morrison writes, "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom" (3). The opening
sentence's blunt declarative syntax — subject, copula, adjective — performs
the very factuality it describes, presenting the house's haunting not as
supernatural anomaly but as settled domestic reality. The word "venom," with
its connotations of both poison and speech (Latin venenum), collapses the
boundary between harm and expression, suggesting that the ghost's malice
is itself a form of utterance demanding interpretation.
```

### Block Quotations

- **Prose**: quotations longer than **4 lines** must be set as block quotes
- **Poetry**: quotations longer than **3 lines** must be set as block quotes
- Indented **0.5 inches** from left margin
- No quotation marks around block quotes
- Period comes **before** the parenthetical citation for block quotes

```
In the novel's central passage, Morrison renders Sethe's recognition:

      She had not thought to ask him and it bothered her still that it might
      have been possible — Loss. Not a thing, but a feeling: a long time.
      A forever kind of thing. (Morrison 89)

The shift from concrete noun ("Loss") to abstract temporal marker ("A forever
kind of thing") enacts the very process of mourning it describes.
```

In LaTeX:
```latex
\begin{quote}
She had not thought to ask him and it bothered her still that it might
have been possible --- Loss. Not a thing, but a feeling: a long time.
A forever kind of thing. (Morrison 89)
\end{quote}
```

## Theoretical Framework

When engaging critical theory, cite canonical texts precisely:

### Commonly Referenced Theorists

| Theorist      | Key Concepts                          | Canonical Citation                          |
|---------------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Foucault      | Discourse, power/knowledge, biopolitics| *Discipline and Punish* (1975), *History of Sexuality* Vol. 1 (1976) |
| Derrida       | Differance, deconstruction, trace     | *Of Grammatology* (1967), *Writing and Difference* (1967) |
| Butler        | Performativity, gender trouble        | *Gender Trouble* (1990), *Bodies That Matter* (1993) |
| Habermas      | Public sphere, communicative action   | *Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* (1962) |
| Said          | Orientalism, contrapuntal reading     | *Orientalism* (1978), *Culture and Imperialism* (1993) |
| Spivak        | Subaltern, strategic essentialism     | "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988) |
| Spillers      | Flesh/body distinction                | "Mama's Baby, Papa's Maybe" (1987) |
| Hartman       | Scenes of subjection, afterlife of slavery | *Scenes of Subjection* (1997) |
| Ahmed         | Affect, orientation, complaint        | *The Cultural Politics of Emotion* (2004) |
| Ranciere      | Distribution of the sensible          | *The Politics of Aesthetics* (2000) |

- Always cite the specific edition/translation you are using
- Note the translator for translated works
- Engage theory as a tool for analysis, not as decoration

## Archival Research Citations

For history and cultural studies papers drawing on archives:

### Chicago Footnote Format for Archival Sources

```
1. W.E.B. Du Bois to Joel Spingarn, 28 October 1914, Joel E. Spingarn
Papers, box 95-3, folder 77, Moorland-Spingarn Research Center, Howard
University, Washington, DC.

2. "Minutes of the Executive Committee," 14 March 1921, NAACP Records,
group I, box C-72, folder "Board Minutes 1921," Library of Congress,
Washington, DC.

3. Photograph, "March on Washington," 28 August 1963, Visual Materials,
lot 13161-19, Prints and Photographs Division, Library of Congress.
```

Structure: **Author/Creator, Title/Description, Date, Collection Name, Box X, Folder Y, Repository, City.**

### Abbreviations in Subsequent Citations

```
First use:
1. National Association for the Advancement of Colored People Records
(hereafter NAACP Records), group I, box C-72, Library of Congress.

Subsequent:
2. NAACP Records, group I, box C-100.
```

## LaTeX Setup (When Required)

Most humanities journals accept Word documents. When LaTeX is required or preferred:

```latex
\documentclass[12pt]{article}
\usepackage[margin=1in]{geometry}
\usepackage{setspace}
\doublespacing
\usepackage[T1]{fontenc}
\usepackage{times}                % or \usepackage{palatino}
\usepackage[utf8]{inputenc}
\usepackage{csquotes}             % smart quotation marks
\usepackage{hyperref}

% For MLA style:
\usepackage[style=mla-new]{biblatex}
\addbibresource{references.bib}

% For Chicago footnotes:
\usepackage[style=chicago-notes]{biblatex}
\addbibresource{references.bib}

\begin{document}
\begin{center}
{\Large\bfseries Title of Paper}\\[1em]
Author Name\\
Institutional Affiliation\\
\end{center}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent Your 150-200 word abstract here as a single paragraph...
\end{abstract}

% Body text (no section numbers by default in many humanities journals)
\section*{Introduction}
...

\printbibliography
\end{document}
```

- **12pt font**, double-spaced throughout
- **1-inch margins** on all sides
- Times New Roman or Palatino preferred
- Section headings often unnumbered (use `\section*{}`)

## Figures and Illustrations

- Most humanities papers contain **no figures**
- Exceptions: art history, visual culture, film studies, digital humanities
- When included, figures are called "Illustrations" or "Plates"
- Image permissions must be obtained and credited
- Fair use applies for critical commentary but has limits for reproduction

```latex
\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{fig_painting.jpg}
\caption{Kara Walker, \textit{A Subtlety} (2014), installation view.
  Domino Sugar Refinery, Brooklyn, NY. Photo: Jason Wyche. Courtesy
  of the artist and Sikkema Jenkins \& Co.}
\label{fig:walker}
\end{figure}
```

## Peer Review

| Journal                  | Review Type    |
|--------------------------|----------------|
| PMLA                     | Double-blind   |
| Critical Inquiry         | Single-blind   |
| New Literary History     | Single-blind   |
| American Historical Review | Double-blind  |
| Signs                    | Double-blind   |
| Cultural Anthropology    | Double-blind   |
| Journal of Philosophy    | Double-blind   |

- Double-blind: remove author name, institutional affiliation, self-citations that reveal identity
- Single-blind: reviewers are anonymous; author identity is known
- Response time: typically 3-6 months (longer than STEM)

## Grant Acknowledgment

Place acknowledgments at the **end of the paper**, before the notes/bibliography:

```
Acknowledgments

This research was supported by a fellowship from the National Endowment
for the Humanities (grant no. FA-123456-20). Earlier versions of this
essay were presented at the 2025 MLA Annual Convention. I am grateful to
[names] for their comments on earlier drafts, and to the archivists at
[repository] for their assistance.
```

- In double-blind review submissions, **omit** acknowledgments or replace with "[Acknowledgments removed for review]"
- Always acknowledge funding sources, even if the journal does not require it
- Acknowledge archivists, librarians, and interlocutors by name

## Writing Conventions

### Tone and Voice

- First person ("I argue") is standard and expected in most humanities journals
- Avoid hedging excessively — make strong, arguable claims
- Passive voice is acceptable but active voice is preferred
- Present tense for discussing texts ("Morrison writes," not "Morrison wrote")
- Past tense for historical events ("Du Bois traveled to Berlin in 1892")

### Common Pitfalls

- Do NOT use IMRAD structure — humanities papers are argument-driven
- Do NOT begin with "In this paper, I will..." — open with the problem or stakes
- Do NOT use bullet points or numbered lists in the body text
- Do NOT overuse block quotations — integrate evidence into your prose
- Do NOT drop quotations without analysis ("quote and run")
- Do NOT treat theory as self-evident; explain how concepts do analytical work
- Do use topic sentences that advance the argument, not merely describe content
- Do engage with specific counterarguments, not strawmen
- Do include a "so what?" — connect your reading to broader intellectual stakes
- Do proofread for consistent citation format (MLA vs. Chicago errors are common)
